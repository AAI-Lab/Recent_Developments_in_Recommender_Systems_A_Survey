Abstract

In this technical survey, the latest advancements in the field of recommender systems are comprehensively summarized. The objective of this study is to provide an overview of the current state-of-the-art in the field and highlight the latest trends in the development of recommender systems. It starts with a comprehensive summary of the main taxonomy of recommender systems, including personalized and group recommender systems. In addition, the survey analyzes the robustness, data bias, and fairness issues in recommender systems, summarizing the evaluation metrics used to assess the performance of these systems. Finally, it provides insights into the latest trends in the development of recommender systems and highlights the new directions for future research in the field.

Personalized Recommender Systems

The emergence of deep learning and the vast availability of online data has made personalized recommender systems more prevalent, significantly enhancing the ease and efficiency with which people discover new experiences. These systems are commonly categorized into three main types: Collaborative Filtering-based, Context-aware-based and Hybrid. Recommender systems have evolved to integrate external knowledge, resulting in knowledge-based recommender systems and the leveraging of LLMs to enhance recommendation efficiency. This paper aims to delve deeper into these advancements, analyzing knowledge-based and LLM-based recommender systems, respectively.

Filtering Based RS

Collaborative filtering (CF) leverages the collective knowledge of a large pool of users to predict an individual user's preferences or opinions, based on the assumption that users who share similar preferences and behaviors will have similar opinions. This method is driven by user profiles based on historical interactions, such as browsing history, past purchases, or songs played previously. CF has memory-based and model-based approaches. The input to the recommendation scoring function is the user interaction data with an item (e.g., user-item ratings or interactions).

Memory-based CF

Memory-based CF utilizes user or item similarities for recommendations.
Recent research on Memory-based CF has focused on refining memory-based CF by exploring new similarity measures, addressing scalability challenges, and considering their integration into hybrid recommendation systems. Memory-based CF, while not the sole focus of research, still plays a valuable role in providing personalized recommendations, particularly with smaller datasets or when simplicity and interpretability are prioritized.

Model-based CF

Model-based CF predicts user preferences based on relationships between them and items. With the success of Deep Learning and Graph Neural Network, there is increasing interest in the application of neural networks.

Model-based CF enhances recommendation accuracy by developing various models to extract in-depth insights from user-item interaction data. Despite significant advancements in model-based CFs, there remains a need for new models to augment the interpretability, computational efficiency, and RS accuracy. It is worth noting that the links of graphs offer a convenient option for mining deep information through multi-hop for the interaction data between users and items. As a result, the GNN will probably become the mainstay of subsequent research.

Context-aware-based CF

To enhance the decision-making capabilities of an RS, it is important to consider contextual information such as time, location, social status, mood, weather, day type, and language. User activity and contextual information are closely intertwined, with reciprocal effects on each other. Unlike memory-based and model-based CF recommendation algorithms, context-aware-based CF requires user and item descriptive information as input. Contextual information can be sourced such as from smart devices providing geolocation data and companion information, or directly from users through their interests.

Recent advancements in contextual information, facilitated by the application of statistical, data mining, and particularly artificial intelligence-based methods, have enabled proactive collection by analyzing user activities within the system, eliminating reliance on self-reported data. Contextual features typically exhibit greater complexity and dynamism than user-item interaction data. For instance, weather conditions, encompassing variables like temperature, humidity, wind, and precipitation, change over time across various scales. The context derived from smart devices is even more intricate, encompassing sensors such as accelerometers, gyroscopes, magnetometers, GPS, and biometric sensors, potentially amounting to around 600 variables. Incorporating this information into a recommender system poses a significant challenge.

Table 2 categorizes relevant work. Context-aware-based CF requires datasets to provide context information, including descriptive information of users and items, so that it can be mined to assist recommendations. Context information is difficult to combine with deep learning, and will be a focus of future research.


Content Based RS

Content-based RS treats recommendations as a user-specific classification problem by learning a classifier based on item features, and provides a more personal recommendation. The RS uses two types of information, the user preference model and user interaction history, to obtain the features of items. Text data are an important source of feature learning.

A notable advantage of Content-based RS is that it allows for item recommendations solely based on their content, without user ratings. Side information can be metadata or user-generated content, and traditionally consists of descriptions of items or full-text indexing of textual items. Content-based RS has evolved to incorporate a wide range of techniques and data sources, including deep learning, sentiment analysis, and Heterogeneous Information Network. These advancements have contributed to the development of more accurate and personalized recommendation systems that take into account not only the content but also the context and user preferences, ultimately improving the user experience.


Knowledge-based RS

Different from CF-based and content-based RS, knowledge-based RS does not rely on user-item interaction history and is commonly used in complex domains where items are not frequently purchased, such as cars, in which users may have constraints such as a budget. The key factor is domain knowledge, which can include users, items, and their interaction. Therefore, the input to the recommendation scoring function requires knowledge in addition to the interaction and description information of users and items.

Knowledge-based RS can use both external knowledge and knowledge generated from datasets, which helps to overcome challenges such as cold starts and grey sheep Figure 2 shows that domain knowledge can be located on both the user and item sides.

Knowledge-based RS has garnered significant attention due to its versatility in a wide range of domains. Knowledge-based RS demonstrate the versatility and potential of knowledge-based RS in different domains, such as e-commerce, healthcare, academia, and software development. These applications demonstrate the potential of integrating domain knowledge into RS to make more informed and personalized recommendations.

Knowledge Graph-based RS

Knowledge graph-based RS is a sub-class of knowledge-based RS that leverages the information contained in a knowledge graph to make better recommendations. The knowledge graph is a collection of entities and their relationships, including objects, relations, and situations. It provides additional information about the recommender system and enhances the quality of recommendations.

The application of knowledge graphs in RS has gained significant attention in recent years and thus become a popular research topic. By integrating the knowledge graph into the recommender system, more evidence can be gathered to improve the quality of recommendations. Knowledge graphs include YAGO, Freebase, DBpedia, NELL, and Wikidata, which cover a wide range of domains. Specialized knowledge graphs include Bio2RDF in biology, Knowlife for biomedical information, and Satori for production. Table 3 provides further details on these knowledge graphs. A knowledge graph is made up of entity-relation-entity triples (h,r,t), with head h, relation r, and tail t, and can be utilized in three ways in a recommender system: knowledge graph embedding, path pattern learning, and hybrid embedding. Existing knowledge can be encoded into a higher-level representation, knowledge graph embedding (KGE).

Knowledge graph-based RS offers a promising avenue for improving the quality and relevance of recommendations by tapping into a wealth of structured knowledge. As the field continues to evolve, researchers are exploring innovative ways to harness knowledge graphs and enhance the performance of recommendation systems across various domains.

Path-based RS

Path-based RS leverages the connectivity patterns between entities in a knowledge graph to enrich the side information of users or items, by evaluating their semantic similarity through different meta-paths. There are three types of entity similarity: User-User, Item-Item, and User-Item.

Path-based method has been widely applied in recommendation systems to leverage the connectivity patterns in the knowledge graph for better recommendation results. Different models have used different similarity evaluation methods, such as PathSim, Hetesim, and LSTM, to calculate the semantic similarity between entities and generate recommendations.


LLM-based RS

With the advancement of Generative Pretraining Transformer (GPT) technology, large-scale language models (LLMs) such as Bard, ChatGPT, and LLaMa have appeared. LLM-based RS must be fine-tuned on the trained LLM, based on the interaction and description of users and items. Conversational recommendation systems that generate recommendations through dialogue have gained prominence.

Due to the huge knowledge capacity and human-like interaction ability of LLM, it can improve the accuracy and interpretability of an RS, and to a certain extent eliminate the cold-start problem. However, the current LLM model is relatively expensive, and the training cost is high; hence, a lightweight LLM is needed.


Hybrid RS

The development of recommender systems has increased their complexity, particularly in practical applications. To achieve real-time, accurate recommendations, these systems often require the integration of various data sources. Contextual information includes features such as historical behavior, social connections, and personal characteristics, highlighting the importance of incorporating multiple data sources for effective recommendations.



Group Recommender Systems

Group recommendation systems provide recommendations to groups of users with similar preferences. A group may have shared interests or may be in the same environment. Group recommendation has both memory-based and model-based approaches. A recommendation must be based on the interaction data of groups, items, and users. Interaction among the group can be minimal or nonexistent in the memory-based approach, and only preferences are aggregated. The modelbased approach seeks a comprehensive representation of a group using machine learning and generally shows better recommendation performance.

Bundle recommendation leverages interaction data encompassing bundles, individual items, and user behavior through deep learning. While similar, group recommendation systems suggest items to a group of users with shared preferences or interests, aiming to meet their collective needs, while bundle systems recommend predefined packages or sets of items, considering the synergy or co-occurrence within them.

Memory-based Approach

Preference aggregation is the predominant method in group recommendation systems. This approach is typically categorized into result aggregation and profile aggregation methods. Result aggregation is to aggregate all users’ recommendation results to form a comprehensive group recommendation. Profile aggregation generates recommendations for a virtual user, an abstraction derived from the group.

Various aggregation strategies are available for group recommendations, each with unique advantages and limitations. The appropriateness of a strategy is contingent on the degree of similarity or diversity in group preferences.


Model-based Approach

Model-based approaches utilize machine learning techniques, including information fusion, game theory, and probabilistic models, to distill relevant features from user interactions. Notably, deep learning has emerged as a vital element in group recommendation, enhancing recommendation performance through the integration of external features such as social attributes, group descriptive characteristics, and common knowledge.

Despite the numerous deep learning-based methods proposed for group recommendation, the recommendation performance remains unsatisfactory due to the sparse and diverse preferences of group users. Further research is needed to address these challenges and enhance the recommendation performance.


Bundle Recommendation

Bundled recommendations can reduce the time spent by users, and increase revenue by selling two or more items as a whole. A typical example occurred in the 1980s when Walmart displayed diapers and beer together. Group and bundled recommendations have commonalities, with bundled recommendations essentially suggesting a combination of items to users. Due to dataset limitations, bundle recommendation can only give recommendations based on the interaction information of users, items, and bundles.

New deep learning models are needed to extend the expression of their relationship and mine intrinsic connections in the data, to improve the accuracy of recommendations. The generation of a specified number of bundles is a future research direction.



Problems in Recommender System

Recommender systems face challenges in terms of robustness, data bias, and fairness. Robustness is the ability to withstand adversarial attacks and eliminate the adverse effects of bias. Adversarial attacks can take different forms, such as manipulating data, model parameters, or the output of an RS. Data bias results in a skewed snapshot of user preferences, causing suboptimal recommendations. This can include popularity, selection, exposure, and position bias. Fairness includes user- and project-based fairness. In the case of userbased fairness, results are not affected by attributes such as race or gender. Similar items of the same category should have equal chances of recommendation. Typical fairness problems include cold-start and long-tail problems.

This survey aims to deliver a comprehensive overview of the challenges encountered by RS in terms of robustness and fairness. It discusses a range of existing methods and techniques devised to tackle these challenges, evaluating their respective strengths and limitations. The paper provides insights into the current state of the field and suggests potential future directions for research in this area.

Robustness

Adversarial attacks have become a popular method for validating the robustness of recommender systems. Researchers often add perturbations to pixel values or text in image processing or natural language processing systems. In a recommender system, an item or user can be represented as an embedding, which provides more attack options, including the use of information such as user and item profiles and embeddings, as well as side information.

There are both black-box and white-box attacks. In a white-box attack, the attacker has access to the model information and uses gradient-based methods to search for adversarial perturbations. In a black-box attack, the attacker lacks this information, and uses methods such as model substitution or DeepFool. The right figure illustrates an attack on a referral RS through the addition of a fake user or item.

In a Data Poisoning Attack (DPA), also known as a shilling or injection attack, the attacker knows the data but not the model information. The goal is to inject fake users and ratings to change the recommendation list. Although traditional association rule- and graph-based algorithms have been used to solve the DPA problem, few of these attacks perform well.

Attacks on recommender systems can be targeted or nontargeted. Targeted attacks aim to mislead the system from the true class to a specific incorrect one. Non-targeted attacks aim to compromise a model toward any class except the actual one. While less explored than targeted attacks, they are critical in the assessment of robustness. Targeted attacks are often driven by explicit motives, while non-targeted attacks can undermine the accuracy of recommendations.

Defense models, such as adversarial training and model distillation, are often used to improve robustness. Adversarial training is generally a min-max game between an adversary and a model.

In conclusion, adversarial training and model distillation are two commonly used defense models to enhance the robustness of recommender systems. These methods address the challenge of robustness by adding perturbations and transferring knowledge, respectively.


Data Bias

Data bias in machine learning refers to the tendency and bias in a model due to imbalanced training data. Acquiring user behavior data for recommender systems through observation rather than experiments creates unavoidable deviations due to various factors. Neglecting these deviations during RS design can inhibit model performance, affecting user experience and trust. Hence, eliminating bias is a crucial research area. RS bias can arise from popularity bias, selection bias and exposure bias. The right figure an example of selection bias.

Popularity bias, also known as an item-based fairness issue or a long-tail problem, occurs when a limited number of highly popular items frequently interact with users, overshadowing lesser-known items. This leads to an inclination to recommend these items, reducing personalization and causing unfair recommendations. Methods to address popularity bias are discussed in Section IV-C.

Selection bias refers to deviations caused by a user’s inability to completely rate each item. Users tend to rate products they are interested in, or are very satisfied or dissatisfied with. Bias occurs because most products do not fall into these categories.

Exposure bias reflects that most users have limited time and can only see and interact with a portion of the items in the system. Other items are unknown to the user, and there are items that the user really likes or dislikes; hence, certain deviations will occur.

This paper contains a discussion of various forms of data bias, namely, popularity bias, selection bias, and exposure bias. To mitigate these biases, researchers mainly rely on debiasing techniques, in addition to utilizing score calibration losses and propensity scores to improve model efficiency. These approaches aim to increase user confidence and trust in the system by ensuring data fairness and reducing discriminatory outcomes.


Fairness

Fairness in recommender systems can be user- or projectbased. In the former case, the system should not discriminate against users because of their sensitive attributes, and in the latter case, each item should have an equal chance of recommendation. Referring to the right figure, cold-start and long-tail problems are typical cases of item-based fairness problems. The long-tail problem is also related to exposure bias.

User-Based Fairness

Mainstream methods are mainly based on meta-learning, confrontation training, federated learning, reinforcement learning, and Transformer.

It can be concluded that researchers are actively exploring different approaches to improve user-based fairness in machine learning models. Metalearning, adversarial training, differential privacy, and multiarmed bandit algorithms are being used to address fairness issues and protect user privacy. Additionally, models like the Cold-Transformer are being developed to adaptively incorporate context-based embeddings to improve the accuracy of user preferences. Overall, these studies demonstrate the importance of addressing user-based fairness in machine learning models and the potential benefits of implementing these approaches to improve service for under-served users while maintaining user privacy.

Item-Based Fairness

Various methods have been proposed to address item-based fairness. Some approaches is based on adversarial training. Meta-learning and reinforcement learning are also used for item-based fairness.

In conclusion, various methods have been proposed for item-based fairness in RS, including causal inference, adversarial training, meta-learning, and reinforcement learning. Each method has its strengths and weaknesses, and the choice of method depends on the specific requirements of the RS system.



Measurements

There are different kinds of evaluation measurements for recommender systems, depending on the task. These can be categorized as Rating Based Indicators (RBIs), and Item Based Indicators (IBIs). An RBI rates the output of a recommender system. A good recommendation can be inferred from a predicted rating that is close to the truth. An IBI considers the output of a recommender system as a set or list of items. A good recommendation is based on the user’s desired match between the predicted item and the target item.



Conclusion

In conclusion, this survey comprehensively reviewed developments in recommender systems, a field that has seen significant recent progress. The diversity of recommender systems and their applications, combined with growing data volume and computing power, has created a rich, dynamic research environment. Recommender systems and Sentiment Analysis are two crucial applications of algorithms, and Malandri et al. have conducted a bibliometric analysis of research articles using structural topic modeling. In the past two decades, research focused on soft computing has shifted from recommender systems to sentiment analysis, and future research in this area is expected to use fuzzy logic for aspect-based sentiment analysis or sentiment knowledge-based reasoning.

This research started with an overview of current trends in personalized and group-based recommender systems. Regarding the former, conventional categorization has been expanded to encompass knowledge-based and LLM-based systems, which have witnessed a significant increase in popularity following recent advancements in deep learning. Personalized recommender system is categorized as collaborative filtering, content-based, knowledge-based, LLM-based, or hybrid.

This work considered the concept of group-based recommender systems, distinguishing between memory-based and model-based techniques. Item-oriented group recommendations, notably bundled recommendations, were discussed, and common challenges encountered by recommender systems were addressed, including robustness, data bias, and fairness. The performance metrics were analyzed based on ratings, ranking, and similarity.

The rich and dynamic research environment of recommender systems has been created by the diversity of its applications and the growing amount of data and computing resources available. Although the field still faces challenges, such as robustness, data bias, and fairness issues, the future of recommender systems holds great promise and the potential to greatly benefit the human race.
